RabbitHole

Inspired by working with Google AI Studio

A spatial learning interface that treats understanding as terrain — something you descend into, branch across, and return from with a clearer sense of the whole.

Role
Lead Designer
Timeline
1 week
Platform
Mobile, Web
Tools
Figma, Origami, After Effects
Thesis

Most learning interfaces flatten curiosity into search bars, menus, and endless lists. RabbitHole starts from a different belief: understanding is spatial, and people learn best when they can move through ideas the way they move through a landscape. Instead of treating learning as a sequence of isolated results, it treats it as a terrain you can descend into, branch across, and return from with a clearer sense of the whole.

Image placeholder
Human behavior

When people are genuinely curious, they rarely move in a straight line. They dig deeper, branch sideways, compare, backtrack, and occasionally zoom out to reconnect the pieces. RabbitHole takes that behavior seriously and turns it into the core interaction model. The goal is not to force people into a perfect path, but to give shape to the messy, recursive way people actually think.

Interaction grammar

The system is built around a small set of spatial actions that make curiosity feel legible. Down means go deeper into the current idea. Left and right mean explore adjacent paths, related questions, or alternate angles. Up means resurface, synthesize, or return to the bigger picture. Hold, drag, and tap are the physical gestures that make those choices feel deliberate. This grammar gives the interface a consistent logic without making it feel rigid, because each movement is simple but still meaningful.

Prototype ladder

The prototype works across three levels of resolution. At the smallest scale, a single interaction shows how a node is chosen and how the camera follows that choice. At the workflow level, the user moves through a chain of connected questions, with each step revealing a new branch of understanding.

At the system level, the whole experience opens into a cave-like map that shows depth, direction, and progress at once. That ladder matters because it lets the idea be understood both as a moment of motion and as a larger learning system — readable instantly, while still holding up as a broader product concept.

Interface logic

RabbitHole does not simply generate more content. It responds to the shape of curiosity. The interface decides what to surface next based on where the user is, how deep they've gone, and whether they are looking for clarification, comparison, or a deeper descent.

That makes each step feel earned rather than arbitrary. The user is not just clicking through content; they are moving through a structure that adapts to the kind of thinking they are doing.

Motion as meaning

One of the most important parts of the concept is that motion is not decorative. The animation is doing conceptual work. When the camera follows a path and recenters on a node, it is not just a transition — it is a statement that the new idea has become the present focus. When the map zooms back out, it is not just an overview effect — it is a reminder that every question sits inside a larger field of related ideas. The motion helps the user understand where they are, what just happened, and what kind of move is possible next.

Background as excavation

Rather than dragging the card itself, the user excavates the surrounding background. That distinction matters because it shifts the interaction from object manipulation to environmental discovery. The user is not moving a result around the screen; they are carving a path through the space between ideas so light can reach the next node. That makes progression feel uncovered rather than triggered. The interface becomes something you reveal your way through, not something you simply operate.

Map and viewport

At the system level, the experience becomes a map with a movable viewport rectangle — a foggy, glass-like frame that acts as a lens over the larger knowledge landscape. The rectangle does not just indicate where the user is; it creates a feeling of focus, as if the user is moving clarity across a field of haze. As the viewport moves, the surrounding map shifts in contrast and legibility, and the next node becomes easier to read as it enters focus. That gives navigation a tactile, almost exploratory quality. The user is not jumping between screens. They are repositioning their attention inside a living structure.

Visual language

The visual direction reinforces the same idea. The interface stays dark, quiet, and atmospheric, with clarity used sparingly as a reward for movement. The cave metaphor helps the system feel deep rather than flat, but the goal is not to make it gritty or literal.

The strongest version of the experience feels clean, spatial, and slightly mysterious — like a map that is still being discovered rather than fully revealed. The visual system supports the interaction instead of competing with it.

What worked

What worked best in the concept was the sense of movement with intention. The user is not being dropped into content; they are carving a path through it. The combination of directional grammar, camera follow, and focus-based map navigation gives the experience a strong internal logic. That logic makes the system feel thoughtful, because every gesture seems to mean something.

What to watch out for

What felt riskier was over-animating the spatial model. If the motion becomes too elaborate, the experience could start to feel unstable instead of grounded. A learning interface still needs orientation, especially if it is asking people to explore. The best version of RabbitHole is the one that makes motion feel alive while preserving a clear sense of place. It should feel like discovery, not disorientation.

"Interfaces do not need to be flatter, faster, or more efficient to be better. Sometimes they need to feel more like places."

Reflection

The larger opportunity here is not just a new navigation pattern. It is a different way of thinking about how digital learning can behave. Most interfaces try to become faster, flatter, and more efficient. RabbitHole asks a different question: what if interfaces could feel more like places? What if understanding was something you entered, moved through, and gradually mapped for yourself? That shift changes the entire emotional register of the product. Learning stops feeling like retrieval and starts feeling like exploration.

What worked best in the concept was the sense of movement with intention: the feeling that the user is not being dropped into content, but carving a path through it. What felt riskier was over-animating the spatial model, which could make the experience feel unstable instead of grounded. The strongest version of RabbitHole is the one that preserves orientation while still making exploration feel alive.

Interfaces do not need to be flatter, faster, or more efficient to be better. Sometimes they need to feel more like places.
