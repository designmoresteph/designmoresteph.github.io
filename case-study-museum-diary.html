I've stood in front of a painting for ten minutes, photographed it, read the placard twice, and still walked out with nothing I could actually remember.

The problem isn't attention. It's friction.

What if logging a piece of art could feel as easy as looking at it?

Problem Framing

Museum visits are oddly ephemeral for something so intentional. You photograph the art, photograph the placard, then spend the next three weeks with 200 unlabeled images in your camera roll. No titles, no artists, no context. What you actually saw collapses into a vague warm feeling.

The existing workarounds are wrong for the moment. Notes apps are for typing, not looking. Your camera roll is storage, not memory. Museum apps are built for orientation, not keeping.

Nobody should have to transcribe a placard by hand in 2025.

The Concept

Museum Diary is built around two things that happen in sequence every time you find a piece you love: Capture and Remember.

Capture is the camera. Point at the artwork, then point at the placard. That's it. Remember is the gallery, a personal collection that grows every visit, organized the way you think rather than the way a database does.

Capture handles the what. Remember handles the why it mattered.

Capture

The two-photo workflow

Artwork first, placard second. The camera knows which is which. After the placard shot, Apple's Vision framework reads the text on-device and fills in title, artist, date, and medium automatically. Done in under ten seconds.

Real-time text detection

While you're framing the placard, green boxes appear over detected text in the viewfinder — OCR you can see working, not a black box that fires after you've already moved on.

Background cleanup on the artwork shot

Gallery photos are never clean. An elbow crosses the frame, a neighboring piece edges in, the floor catches light. What lands in your collection is the piece itself, not the room around it.

Location-aware logging

The app detects which museum you're in. The artwork tags itself to the right visit, city, and trip — not you.

Remember

The gallery, sorted by time

Your collection reads as a timeline — newest visits at the top, grouped by day and museum. Every afternoon you spent in a gallery is still there, in order.

The artwork view

The placard sits bottom-right — where a real museum label would be, not in a tooltip. The information accompanies the art rather than interrupting it.

Color extraction

Every artwork gets a five-color palette pulled from the image. A small thing that makes the collection feel alive and makes your taste legible at a glance.

The profile

Total artworks, artists, centuries spanned, cities visited. Built to be the screen you actually want to share, not a dashboard you close.

Design Details

The placard is part of the image, not a tooltip

In the artwork detail view, the placard overlay sits bottom-right against the full-bleed photo. Warm off-white, soft shadow — it mimics what you'd see standing in front of the real thing. The OCR logic runs three tiers: keyword detection first, then pattern matching, then positional heuristics. If it still fails, you get two retries before manual entry. The app never leaves you stuck.

Remove friction the user didn't know was there

The camera detects your museum so you don't have to tag it. Background removal cleans the artwork photo so you don't have to crop it. The app does the boring work quietly. The user just sees that their collection looks good and is organized correctly.

Reflection

I started this because I kept losing the things I found in museums. Not the experience, but the specifics. The tiny details that make a piece yours: the medium, the exact date, the thing you noticed.

If it works, you leave a museum and your phone already knows what you saw. The visit becomes part of something instead of disappearing into a camera roll.

"Not logging. Not organization. Keeping."

Building it taught me that the hardest part wasn't the OCR or the camera or the data model. It was figuring out what the app was actually for. Not logging. Not organization. Keeping.

Database: 107,000+ museums worldwide, searchable, with the option to add your own.

What didn't make it (yet): An audio guide mode, a social layer for sharing collections, a museum wishlist trip planner with map integration, and a bench photo gallery (yes, just great museum benches).

Thanks to everyone who let me drag them to museums for "research."
